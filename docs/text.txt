We are suggesting this idea called the Financial Connectome, which is like a new way to look at financial markets by thinking of them as a brain connectome from neuroscience. It gives a framework to analyze how markets behave collectively, drawing from brain science. Even though there have been a lot of advances in modeling finances, we still do not have a good systematic approach to see how group decisions in markets change over different situations or regimes. In neuroscience, it is clear that decisions come from various brain parts interacting, not just one thing, like systems for evaluating value, checking risks, reacting to fear, and keeping control.

From that, we can build the idea of Neural Decision Systems, (NDS), which acts as a key part in the Financial Connectome. These NDS are like different subsystems that interact and represent all sorts of market behaviors, similar to how neural circuits work in the brain. They shift their importance depending on the market regime, which lets stable times, volatile periods, or even crises happen in markets. Instead of seeing markets as one big rational player, this view treats them as a mix of competing and working-together decision systems. It seems like that helps explain shifts between regimes, how markets adapt, and what makes them resilient, though I am not totally sure on the resilience part yet.

This approach connects neuroscience on decisions with financial modeling in a pretty interesting way. It provides a framework that can scale up to understand collective financial choices as economic conditions change. Some people might think it oversimplifies things, but it feels like a step forward.
