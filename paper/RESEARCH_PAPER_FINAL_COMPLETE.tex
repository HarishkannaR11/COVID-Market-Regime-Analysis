\begin{abstract}
We like to think of financial markets as logical systems. In reality, they are driven by a messy mix of reason and emotion. This paper treats the market not as a calculator, but as a collective brain---an "Economic Nervous System." We tackle the classic conflict between neuroscience and finance by mapping market behavior to five biological decision systems: Value, Risk, Sentiment, Insula, and Control. Using the COVID-19 crisis as a natural experiment, we analyzed 1,516 trading days of the NIFTY Bank Index to see if the market's "mind" changed under stress. The results were stark. Post-pandemic, the market didn't just get volatile; it got obsessive. Market states became $24.1\%$ more persistent, and the dominant mood shifted from balanced ($0.00$) to deeply fearful ($-2.01$). Essentially, the market woke up: activation frequencies surged across every cognitive system, with the 'Control' and 'Risk' centers nearly doubling in activity. These metrics suggest the economy transformed into a "Vigilant Guard"---stable, but permanently anxious.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Financial markets are not cold, calculating machines. They are deeply human constructs, mirroring our collective panic and greed. Traditional economics loves the "Rational Agent"---a hypothetical figure who acts with perfect logic---but history tells a different story. Real markets scream, crash, and rally, driven as much by adrenaline as by algebra. This was never more obvious than during the COVID-19 pandemic.

We can view this chaos through the lens of modern neuroscience. Decisions don't come from a single logical circuit. They emerge from a tug-of-war between competing brain systems: some wanting reward (Value), some fearing loss (Risk), and others trying to stay in control. Until now, "Neurofinance" has mostly stayed in the lab.

We want to bring those insights into the wild. By treating the market as a single cognitive entity, we can analyze the NIFTY Bank Index as if it were a patient. The pandemic gives us a perfect "before and after" snapshot. Did the trauma of COVID-19 simply pass, or did it leave a permanent scar on the market's personality?

Using machine learning, we built a "Brain Scanner" for the economy. We tracked how the market's cognitive patterns---its tendency to dwell on risk or chase opportunities---rewired themselves. We weren't just looking for price changes; we were looking for a change in \textit{thinking}.


\subsection{Research Gap}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Traditional finance struggles to explain structural shifts in group decision-making during crises.
    \item Existing neurofinance studies rarely scale to macroscopic market data.
    \item Regime analyses typically focus on price levels or volatility, overlooking the underlying decision dynamics.
    \item It remains unclear whether COVID-19 caused a lasting neuro-cognitive scar on market behavior.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contribution}
This paper connects ideas about brains and markets to help figure out different market situations. We introduce a neurocomputational framework for diagnosing market regimes. While the term sounds complicated, fundamentally, it looks at how markets work through different decision systems. Specifically:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Instead of seeing the market as one big rational thing, we model it as five competing decision systems interacting. This seems like a better way to think about it, since markets are messy with all those players.
    \item We looked at a ton of data, like 1,516 trading days from the NIFTY Bank Index, and compared what happened before and after COVID. By checking out the neural signatures in those periods, we show how that era shift really changed things.
    \item We identify distinct neuro-computational states using engineered features and machine learning to spot these patterns.
    \item The evidence shows the market after the pandemic got more rigid, and also more risk-averse in its structure. It is not as flexible anymore, which stands out because it makes you wonder if that is permanent or just temporary.
\end{itemize}

The main goal here isn't only about predicting prices or modeling them. It is more to get at the psychology behind the economy. We try to understand the human side through this computational lens. Some might say it is oversimplifying, but it could open up new ways to look at economic behavior.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
Drawing from behavioral finance and the biology of stress, we propose three core hypotheses about the Post-COVID market:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$H_1$: The Activation Hypothesis.} We expect the market's ``brain'' to be significantly more active post-crisis. specifically, the Amygdala-like ``Risk'' system should show heightened firing rates, reflecting a shift from complacency to hyper-vigilance.
    
    \item \textbf{$H_2$: The Persistence Hypothesis.} Traumatic stress often reduces flexibility. We hypothesize that market states (whether fear or optimism) will become ``stickier'' in the post-pandemic era, showing significantly longer run lengths and reduced switching.
    
    \item \textbf{$H_3$: The Paradox Hypothesis.} Contrary to the mean-reversion expected in standard finance, we predict the market will not return to its pre-COVID baseline. Instead, it will settle into a ``Risk-Dominant Equilibrium''---stable, yet structurally negative.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Neuro-Computational System Architecture}
The proposed Neuro-Financial Computing Framework (Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture_diagram}) operates as a four-stage pipeline:

\subsubsection{Data Ingestion Layer}
We process 1,516 trading days of the NIFTY Bank Index, treating the Pre-COVID (773 days) and Post-COVID (743 days) eras as distinct diagnostic regimes.

\subsubsection{Neuro-Financial Computing Layer}
Raw data is transformed into 22 financial features acting as proxies for cognitive processes. Indicators like momentum serve as signals for reward-seeking (Greed), while volatility metrics flag threat detection (Risk), effectively translating market noise into clear neuro-computational inputs.

\subsubsection{Classification Layer}
Five independent XGBoost classifiers act as our "neural scanners," predicting the activation (ON/OFF) of each brain system. We chose this non-linear approach because market states, like brain states, are rarely simple linear progressions.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Layer}
Finally, the system synthesizes these outputs into a composite "Neuro-Decision Score" (NDS). We also track how "obsessive" the market becomes by analyzing persistence (Run Length) and flexibility (Switching Frequency) to validate our "Vigilant Guard" hypothesis.

\subsection{Machine Learning Architecture}

For each brain system, XGBoost classifiers predict distinct activation states (ON/OFF). We explicitly defined these target variables using rules inspired by neuroanatomy, mapping specific market behaviors directly to cognitive states.

\textbf{Hyperparameters:} max\_depth=3, learning\_rate=0.05, n\_estimators=200, L1=1.0, L2=2.0, subsample=0.8.

\textbf{Validation:} We strictly employed a 70/30 time-ordered split. Training on earlier days and testing on the future prevents data leakage and ensures our models aren't just memorizing history.

\subsection{Analytical Metrics}

\textbf{Neuro-Decision Score (NDS):}
\begin{equation}
\text{NDS}_t = \sum_{s \in \{V,R,Se,I,C\}} w_s \cdot A_{s,t}
\end{equation}
where $A_{s,t} \in \{0,1\}$ represents the activation state. The weights $w_s$ are assigned based on the system's role in approach (+) or avoidance (-) behavior: Value, Sentiment, and Control are assigned $w=+1$, while Risk and Insula are assigned $w=-1$.

\textbf{Persistence Metrics:} Run length (consecutive same-state days), switching frequency, Shannon entropy.

\section{Data and Preprocessing}

\subsection{Data Description and Rationale}

We use daily NIFTY Bank Index data covering 1,516 trading days split into Pre-COVID (Jan 2017--Mar 2020, 773 days) and Post-COVID (Apr 2020--Feb 2023, 743 days). The Bank index was chosen because it is liquid, economically sensitive, and reflects collective risk and sentiment in the financial sector. Using a single, well-studied index keeps the analysis focused and interpretable while still capturing broad market dynamics.

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Preprocessing}
We construct 22 features across five categories: returns/momentum, volatility, volume, technical indicators, and cross-asset signals. Preprocessing steps include:

- Handling missing values by forward-fill where appropriate and dropping the first rows created by rolling windows.
- Computing rolling volatility and moving averages with fixed windows to capture short- and medium-term dynamics.
- Winsorizing extreme outliers on key features to reduce the influence of rare spikes.
- Standardizing features to zero mean and unit variance separately for Pre- and Post-COVID periods to avoid information leakage across regimes.

\subsection{Data Splits and Distribution}
All modeling preserves temporal order. For each period we use a 70/30 train-test split by time to maintain out-of-sample validity: earlier days for training, later days for testing. We also run robustness checks with alternative splits (65/35, 60/40) and rolling-window validation. Class balance (system ON/OFF labels) varies by system; when necessary we report both absolute activation frequency and relative changes to give a full picture.


\subsection{Model Comparison}

To ensure robust results we compared several classifiers: XGBoost, LightGBM, and Logistic Regression. Tree-based ensembles (XGBoost and LightGBM) consistently outperformed linear models on this task; XGBoost provided the best trade-off between accuracy and stability and was used for the main analysis. Detailed model comparisons are reported in the supplementary material.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Interpretation of Results}

Our "Brain Scan" of the market reveals three critical changes in its psychology:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Obsessive Focus (Increased Persistence):} Pre-COVID, the market would flirt with an idea (like "risk") and then move on. Post-COVID, it obsesses. All decision systems showed significantly longer run lengths, meaning the market now gets "stuck" in its moods.
	\item \textbf{Chronic Anxiety (Risk-Dominant Shift):} The average mood (NDS) collapsed from a neutral 0.00 to a deeply negative -2.01. This isn't just a bad day; it's a personality change. The market has shifted from being an "optimist" to a "pessimist."
	\item \textbf{Loss of Nuance (Reduced Entropy):} The complexity of the market's thoughts decreased. It became more binary, more rigid, and less capable of processing subtle, mixed signals.
\end{itemize}

These findings confirm that the pandemic didn't just bruise the economy; it rewired it.

\subsection{Why This Matters: The Theory}

Our findings challenge the idea that markets always "bounce back" (Mean Reversion). Instead, they support the "Adaptive Markets" view, but with a neuro-computational twist.

\textbf{1. The New Normal is "Anxious":} Just as a person can have a "happy" baseline and a "depressed" baseline, the market has shifted from a Balanced Equilibrium to a Risk-Dominant Equilibrium.

\textbf{2. Economic PTSD (Hysteresis):} The market has "memory." The trauma of 2020 left a permanent mark. Even after the virus threat faded, the neural pathways for "Risk" remained dominant. The Guard dares not sleep again.

\textbf{3. The Survival Instinct:} The priorities changed. In calm times, greed leads. In crisis, fear takes the wheel and \textit{refuses to let go}, creating the paradox of stable anxiety.

\subsection{Practical Applications: What This Means for Investors}

For the average investor and fund manager, the "Vigilant Guard" market requires a change in strategy:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Don't Expect "Normal":} The market won't simply "snap back" to 2019. Strategies betting on a return to calm may fail.
    \item \textbf{Ride the Trend:} When a mood starts, it lasts. Momentum strategies are likely to outperform contrarian ones in this stickier environment.
    \item \textbf{Risk is the Wallpaper:} A negative score doesn't mean a crash is imminent; it means the market is \textit{fragile}. A small piece of bad news could trigger a disproportionate reaction because the "fear center" is already primed.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

While our "Brain Scanner" offers a powerful new perspective, it is a model, not a crystal ball. Our study focuses on a single major index (NIFTY Bank), and while five brain systems capture the core drivers, the real human brain (and market) is infinitely more complex. Finally, while we can diagnose the state, we cannot always predict the specific trigger that will cause the next shift.

\section{Conclusion}

This study successfully applied a "Brain Scanner" to the financial market, achieving 96--98\% accuracy in decoding its hidden states. The data tells a clear story of transformation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Awakening:} COVID-19 woke the market up, doubling the activity of its "fear center."
    \item \textbf{The Shift:} It permanently moved from a balanced mood to a defensive one.
    \item \textbf{The Trap:} It stopped "fidgeting" and became rigid, locking itself into a state of high alert.
\end{itemize}

Ultimately, we have shown that the market transformed from a \textbf{Restless Sleeper} into a \textbf{Vigilant Guard}. This "Economic Nervous System" framework provides a new, powerful way to diagnose the hidden health of our financial economy—not just by looking at prices, but by understanding the psychology driving them.
